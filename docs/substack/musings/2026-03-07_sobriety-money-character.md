# Sobriety Doesn't Fix Your Character  - And Neither Does Money

*Michael's Musings  - March 7, 2026*

---

> 🎨 **IMAGE PROMPT:** *A cracked mirror reflecting two different scenes: on one side a whiskey glass, on the other side stacks of cash. Both sides cracked and distorted. Dark moody lighting, photorealistic, shallow depth of field. The mirror frame is ornate and old.*

Saturday morning AA. The topic was "101 Common Cliches of Alcoholics Anonymous," and Art gets up first. I love when Art shares. He's been sober longer than I've been alive and he still talks like a guy who figured something out yesterday.

Today he talked about people and money. And I can't stop thinking about it.

---

Here's the cliche that hit: **"Alcohol doesn't change who you are when you're sober."**

Most people hear that and nod along. Yeah yeah, liquid courage, inhibitions lowered, whatever. But Art went deeper.

He said the problem isn't that alcohol *makes* you an asshole. The problem is that getting sober doesn't *stop* you from being one. You take the bottle away and you're still the same person with the same character defects. The lying, the selfishness, the shortcuts  - that stuff doesn't evaporate when you put the plug in the jug.

**You have to do the work.** Sobriety is the prerequisite, not the solution.

---

> 🎨 **IMAGE PROMPT:** *A lottery winner holding a giant oversized check, but the photo is aged and yellowed like it's from years ago. Behind them, a "FOR SALE" sign on a mansion. Bittersweet, documentary-style photography.*

And here's where it gets interesting for money.

Look at lottery winners. Study after study shows that people who win millions are broke within 5 years at astronomical rates. We all know this. We shake our heads. "How do you blow $100 million?"

Same way an alcoholic with 30 days sober blows their sobriety. **The money didn't fix the character.**

You give $10 million to someone who doesn't know how to manage $1,000, and they don't suddenly learn. They just make $10 million worth of the same mistakes. Bigger house, same habits. Nicer car, same impulse control. More zeros, same relationship with spending.

---

> 🎨 **IMAGE PROMPT:** *A minimalist trading terminal showing a small account balance of $100, but the screen is surrounded by carefully organized notebooks, charts, and a written money plan. Clean desk, intentional setup. The contrast between the small number and the professional preparation tells the story.*

I see this in trading too. Not with millions  - with hundreds.

I've got $100 in my TastyTrade account right now. Some people would laugh at that. "What are you gonna do with $100?"

Build discipline. That's what.

If I can't manage a $100 account responsibly  - position sizing, stop losses, taking profits, tracking taxes, not revenge trading  - then I have absolutely no business managing $10,000. Or $100,000. The dollar amount is irrelevant. The *character* behind the decisions is everything.

Recovery taught me that. You practice the principles in all your affairs. *All* of them. Not just the ones that feel significant.

---

So here's what I'm doing differently:

1. **I wrote a money plan before I need one.** Compounding rules, withdrawal triggers, charity allocation  - all documented before the account is big enough for it to matter. Because if I wait until the money shows up to figure out what to do with it, I've already lost.

2. **I'm giving first.** 10% of every profit to charity. Not because I'm generous. Because recovery taught me that you can't keep what you don't give away.

3. **I'm building the infrastructure.** Dashboards, tracking, transparent logging. You know why? Because when there's nowhere to hide, you can't lie to yourself.

Art would probably say I'm overcomplicating it. But that's okay. I'm a builder. I process things by building systems.

The point isn't the system. The point is the character underneath it.

---

*If you read this and thought "I should probably write out my money plan too"  - yeah. You should. Right now. Not when you need one. Now.*
